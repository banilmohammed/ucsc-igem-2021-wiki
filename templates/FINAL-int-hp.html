Background

In order to have a clear understanding of the implications of our project, our Human Practices team had to be flexible to pivot points in our research to fully represent our findings. The initial goal of our project was to detect and prevent STEC outbreaks stemming from the consumption of beef and ready-to-eat lettuce. This would keep consumers from falling sick, and promote food safety as the number one priority. Through interviews with food safety experts in leafy green production, vaccine developers, and ex-FDA/USDA officials, we realized that our project had a broader range of applications than initially expected.

STEC

Over 2.8 million people are infected by Shiga-toxin producing E. coli (STEC) every year worldwide (Pires et al., 2014). In 2018 the Salinas Valley romaine lettuce outbreak was responsible for infections in 27 different states (CDC, 2020). Ingesting contaminated food can cause Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS), a type of kidney failure. In the Americas and Europe, beef products are the most impacted by STEC, followed by leafy greens. In order to reduce the burden of STEC-related food recalls, our project has the potential to reform current food safety procedures.
